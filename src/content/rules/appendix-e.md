Names in Nippon carry social meaning. A person's full name — clan name followed by given name — announces their lineage, their region, and their social class to anyone who hears it. A Buke warrior introduces himself as "Ryojii Korisawa" — Ryojii being the clan name, Korisawa the personal name. A Heimin farmer may have only a personal name; a high-ranking court noble may have multiple ceremonial names accumulated over a career.

This appendix provides tools for players to select authentic-feeling names and for Game Masters to generate names quickly during play. Syllable tables allow both deliberate composition and random generation.

---

## How Names Work in Nippon

**Buke (Warrior Class):** Clan Name + Given Name. "Tamekura Borukuro" — Tamekura is the clan, Borukuro is the personal name. Always stated in this order.

**Heimin (Common People):** Usually a single personal name, sometimes with a place or trade appended informally. A farmer named Jiro from Omi might be called "Omi no Jiro" (Jiro of Omi) to distinguish him from other Jiros.

**Religious Names:** Gakusho who take formal vows often abandon their birth name and take a religious name from their tradition. These are single names with a distinctly Buddhist or Shinto character.

**Yakuza and Criminal Names:** Often a single name or nickname. High-ranking Yakuza may combine an area name with a personal name informally: "Namba no Ichibei" (Ichibei of Namba district).

---

## Constructing a Clan Name

Clan names are almost always two syllables combining a first syllable (often a place name, natural feature, or ancestral word) with a second syllable (a common noun or suffix). Examples:

- **Ryojii:** Ryo (a flowing water prefix) + jii (elder stream)
- **Tamekura:** Tame (accumulated) + kura (storehouse/treasury)
- **Kojitomi:** Koji (small path) + tomi (wealth/prosperity)
- **Shibado:** Shiba (brushwood/thicket) + do (path/way)

Many clan names derive from the province or geographic feature associated with the clan's original domain: Yamamoto (base of the mountain), Kawashima (river island), Fujiwara (plain of wisteria).

---

## Constructing a Male Given Name

Male given names for Buke typically use elements that convey virtues or aspirations. Common second-element patterns:

- **-hide** (excellent): Nobuhide, Yoshihide
- **-moto** (origin/foundation): Masamoto, Yorimoto
- **-naga** (long/enduring): Nobunaga, Yoshinaga
- **-yoshi** (good/lucky): Noriyoshi, Takayoshi
- **-hiro** (broad/generous): Yoshihiro, Masahiro
- **-tomo** (friend/companion): Yoshitomo, Noritomo
- **-kage** (shadow — for warriors and advisors): Takakage, Yurikage

---

## Constructing a Female Given Name

Female Buke names often use elements from nature, virtue, or aesthetic concepts. Common suffixes:

- **-ko** (child — respectful for high-born women): Hanako, Yoshiko
- **-e** (branch, beauty): Masae, Yukae
- **-hime** (princess): Yukihime, Tomohime
- **-ka** (a softening suffix): Shizuka, Haruka

Common female name elements: Hana (flower), Yuki (snow/luck), Aki (autumn/bright), Haru (spring), Kiku (chrysanthemum), Ume (plum blossom), Chiyo (a thousand years), Tama (jewel), Shizu (quiet/still).

Single-element names — Hana, Yuki, Aki, Chiyo, Ume — are common for Heimin women and some Buke women.

## Provincial Title Additions

A Samurai who holds a provincial administrative rank may append a title suffix derived from the province name.

| Suffix | Meaning / Use | Example |
|---|---|---|
| -no-Kami | Governor (highest provincial title) | Musashi-no-Kami |
| -no-Suke | Vice-Governor (often ceremonial) | Kotsuke-no-Suke |
| -no-Jo | Third-rank officer (warrior appointment) | Totomi-no-Jo |
| -no-Gonnokami | Acting Governor | Shimosa-no-Gonnokami |
| Nyudo | Taken Buddhist vows while retaining secular power | Ryojii Nyudo Korisawa |
| Dono | Lord (respectful address, any high-ranking Buke) | Ryojii Dono |

## A Note on Authenticity

These name tables produce names that feel authentically Japanese without being transcriptions of actual historical names. Players who want to research actual Japanese historical names for additional inspiration will find rich material — but the tables here are designed so that no knowledge of Japanese is required to produce a name that sounds and feels right in the world of Nippon.

The most important test of a name is whether it sounds like it belongs in the world you are playing in. If combining two table entries produces something awkward, try an adjacent entry or adjust a vowel. Names evolved organically over time and the tables here reflect that organic quality rather than rigid grammatical rules.
