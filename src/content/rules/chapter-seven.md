Nippon is a society built on social performance. Every interaction carries weight — who you are, how you present yourself, what you owe and are owed, whether your conduct matches your station. The social systems in Savage Bushido are not flavor layered over combat mechanics. They are mechanical systems in their own right, with consequences as real as a sword wound.

This chapter covers three interlocking systems: the On honor score that follows every character everywhere, the three-tier social conflict framework that handles interactions from casual conversation to formal petition before a Daimyo, and the Group Rank system that tracks your standing within organizations.

---

## On — The Honor Score

On is a number between 1 and 20 that represents your social reputation — the sum of your conduct, your lineage, your deeds, and the world's perception of all three. It is not purely internal virtue (a secret villain with excellent manners can have high On) nor purely external performance (genuine heroism that no one witnesses gains nothing). On is your reputation as a social fact.

### On and Social Rolls

When your On is known to the person you are dealing with, your On modifier applies to social skill rolls:

| On Score | Social Modifier |
|---|---|
| 1–4 | −2 |
| 5–7 | −1 |
| 8–12 | +0 |
| 13–16 | +1 |
| 17–20 | +2 |

This modifier applies to Socialize, Taunt (with the On inversion rule — see Chapter Three), Intimidation, and Perform rolls made in social contexts. It does not apply when your identity is unknown.

### Starting On

On is set at character creation through Birth and Profession. See Chapter Two for the full table. Most Buke characters begin with On 6–12. Most Heimin begin with On 4–6. Yakuza begin lower; Gakusho begin higher.

### Gaining On

On increases through honorable conduct — actions that the world of Nippon recognizes as befitting or exceeding your station:

| Action | On Gain |
|---|---|
| Completing a sworn duty at personal cost | +1 |
| Victory in a Formal Duel (submission or death terms) | +1 |
| Sparing a defeated opponent in a Formal Duel | +1 |
| Demonstrated act of courage witnessed by peers | +1 |
| Successfully petitioning for Group Rank advancement | +1 |
| Major service to your lord or organization | +1 to +3 |
| Act of exceptional valor in Mass Battle | +1 |
| Completing a significant artistic work (Master quality) | +1 |
| Receiving a formal commendation from a superior | +1 |

### Losing On

On decreases through dishonorable conduct:

| Action | On Loss |
|---|---|
| Breaking a sworn oath | −1d4 |
| Attacking an unarmed or surrendered opponent without cause | −2 |
| Violating Formal Duel terms | −3 |
| Being captured by an enemy | −1 |
| Fleeing combat when allies are fighting | −1 |
| Publicly embarrassing your lord or clan | −1 to −3 |
| Carrying an illegal weapon (witnessed) | −1 |
| Executing a loser when terms were first blood | −1 |
| Committing an act beneath your station | −1 |
| Heimin caught carrying Samurai weapons | −2 |

### On and Death

A Samurai who dies in battle with their On intact has died with honor. Their family's standing is maintained. A character who is captured and executed rather than dying in battle or committing seppuku is considered to have died in disgrace. This distinction matters to surviving family members and shapes how NPCs remember the character.

## The Three-Tier Social Conflict System

Not every social interaction requires the same level of mechanical engagement. Savage Bushido organizes social interactions into three tiers based on stakes and complexity.

### Tier One: Routine Interactions

A single skill roll with the On modifier applied if relevant. No extended mechanic. Used for:

- Asking directions, gathering rumors, purchasing goods
- Casual conversation with NPCs of similar or lower status
- Routine requests to people who have no strong reason to refuse
- Social interactions where failure is inconvenient but not consequential

The GM sets a Target Number. With a success the interaction goes as hoped. With a failure the interaction goes poorly — the NPC is unhelpful, suspicious, or offended. The On modifier applies if the NPC knows who they are dealing with.

### Tier Two: Significant Interactions

The SWADE Social Conflict system from the core rules, with On modifier and Group Rank modifier added. Used for:

- Requesting assistance from someone with reason to be cautious
- Negotiating terms that matter to both parties
- Convincing a superior to act against their initial preference
- Social interactions where failure has meaningful consequences

**On modifier:** Applied to each roll in the Social Conflict sequence.

**Group Rank modifier:** If both characters belong to the same organization, the difference in Group Rank adds to the higher-ranked character's rolls (maximum +3 in either direction).

### Tier Three: High-Stakes Formal Interactions

The Nippon Detailed System — a structured interaction with defined phases, Momentum tracking, and significant On consequences. Used for:

- Petitioning a Daimyo or powerful figure
- Formal tea ceremony as a political event
- A formal apology for a significant transgression
- Treaty negotiation between clans or organizations
- Any interaction where the outcome will change the campaign significantly

## Tier Three: The Nippon Detailed System

### Step One: Establish Social Status

Both parties calculate their Social Status for this interaction:

**Social Status = On modifier + Group Rank modifier**

Group Rank modifier: +1 per rank higher than the other party (maximum +3); −1 per rank lower (maximum −3).

The party with higher Social Status begins with 1 free Momentum.

### Step Two: Set Starting Disposition

The GM sets the target's Starting Disposition based on prior relationship, reputation, and circumstances:

| Disposition | Effect |
|---|---|
| Hostile | Target begins with +2 Momentum |
| Uncooperative | Target begins with +1 Momentum |
| Neutral | No starting modifier |
| Friendly | Petitioner begins with +1 Momentum |
| Helpful | Petitioner begins with +2 Momentum |

### Step Three: Preparation Phase

Before the formal interaction begins, the petitioner may take preparatory actions. Each successful action shifts Disposition one step toward Friendly:

- **Gift (Socialize roll):** A gift appropriate to the occasion and relationship. With a success, +1 Disposition shift. With a raise, +2 shifts. The gift must be appropriate — too modest is an insult; too grand creates awkwardness.
- **Go-between (Socialize or Connections):** A respected intermediary who vouches for the petitioner. Automatic +1 Disposition shift if the intermediary is respected by the target.
- **Prior favor:** If the petitioner has done the target a significant service, +1 to +2 Disposition shifts depending on the magnitude of the favor.
- **Reputation:** If the petitioner's On is 15+, +1 Disposition shift automatically (their reputation precedes them).

### Step Four: The Formal Exchange

Both parties choose an Approach each round and roll the associated skill. The winner of the opposed roll gains 1 Momentum (a raise gains 2). Ties go to the party with higher Social Status.

| Approach | Skill | Best When |
|---|---|---|
| Cooperative | Socialize | Target is Neutral or better |
| Adversarial | Taunt | Pressing an advantage; target is Hostile |
| Authority | Intimidation | You have formal power over the target |
| Artistic | Perform | Tea ceremony; poetry exchange; aesthetic demonstration |
| Scholarly | Knowledge (relevant) | Demonstrating expertise; invoking precedent |

**Invoking authority:** A character who uses Intimidation as their Approach in a formal context is explicitly invoking their formal power over the target. With a success they gain +1 Momentum; with a failure they lose 1 Momentum (the invocation was resented). This approach closes off Cooperative approaches for the remainder of the interaction.

### Step Five: Resolution

The first party to reach the Threshold wins the interaction. The GM sets the Threshold based on the stakes:

| Stakes | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Moderate (a favor, minor concession) | 3 |
| Significant (major support, significant concession) | 5 |
| Landmark (treaty, major political shift, forgiveness of serious transgression) | 7 |

**On consequences of winning and losing:**

| Outcome | On Effect |
|---|---|
| Win at Threshold 5+ | +1 On for the winner |
| Lose at Threshold 5+ | −1 On for the loser |
| Win at Threshold 7 | +2 On for the winner; −2 On for the loser |
| Win through a Raise on the final roll | +1 additional On |

## Five Worked Scenarios

### Scenario A: Petitioning for Group Rank Advancement

**Setup:** A Seasoned Bushi (Group Rank 2 in their clan) petitions their Daimyo (Group Rank 5) for elevation to Group Rank 3. The Bushi has served honorably, completed two significant missions, and prepared a modest gift.

**Social Status:** Bushi On 12 (+0 modifier) + Group Rank 2 = effective modifier 0. Daimyo On 16 (+1) + Group Rank 5 = +4 effective. The Daimyo begins with 1 free Momentum.

**Starting Disposition:** The Daimyo begins Neutral (the Bushi has served well but advancement is not given freely).

**Preparation:** The Bushi presents a gift (Good quality sake, appropriate to the occasion). Socialize roll, success — Disposition shifts to Friendly. The Daimyo begins with +1 Momentum (Friendly) rather than +1 from Social Status — take the higher, so still +1.

**Threshold:** 5 (significant — Group Rank advancement is a meaningful concession).

**The exchange:** The Bushi uses Cooperative approach (Socialize) — appropriate to the respectful petition context. The Daimyo uses Authority (they are evaluating, not petitioning). Three rounds of exchange, the Bushi gains Momentum steadily. Reaches 5. The petition is granted.

**Outcome:** Bushi advances to Group Rank 3. +1 On for the successful petition. The Daimyo gains nothing mechanically — they made a judgment in their own interest.

---

### Scenario B: The Tea Ceremony Confrontation

**Setup:** Two rival clan representatives meet for a formal tea ceremony. Both know the ceremony is cover for a negotiation about contested border territory. The host has a significant advantage in social status (On 15 vs. On 10); the guest has a skilled Perform d10 and brought a renowned tea master as go-between.

**Social Status:** Host On 15 (+1) + Group Rank 3 = +4. Guest On 10 (+0) + Group Rank 2 = +2. Host begins with 1 free Momentum.

**Starting Disposition:** Neutral (rivals, but present at the table voluntarily).

**Preparation:** The guest's tea master go-between shifts Disposition one step to Friendly for the guest's side. The host's Reputation (On 15) automatically shifts Disposition one step back to Neutral. Net result: Neutral, but the guest has demonstrated the quality of their connections.

**The ceremony as Tier 3:** Because the ceremony uses all three Art layers (Craft for the tea preparation, Knowledge for the object selection, Perform for the ceremony itself), and the guest achieves Good quality on each, the tea ceremony itself counts as one Artistic approach at +2 Momentum (the rare case where preparation converts directly to Momentum).

**Threshold:** 5 (significant border concession).

**Outcome:** The guest's Perform advantage carries the day despite the social status gap. The border is conceded. −1 On for the host; +1 On for the guest.

---

### Scenario C: Petitioning the Daimyo — A Difficult Audience

**Setup:** A Ronin (On 6, no Group Rank) needs to petition a Daimyo (On 16, Group Rank 5) for permission to investigate a murder in his territory. The Ronin has no introduction, no gift prepared, and has arrived unexpectedly. The Daimyo's chamberlain has reluctantly allowed an audience.

**Social Status:** Ronin On 6 (−1) + Group Rank 0 = −4 effective. Daimyo On 16 (+1) + Group Rank 5 = +6 effective. The Daimyo begins with 2 free Momentum.

**Starting Disposition:** Uncooperative (+1 Momentum to Daimyo). A Ronin demanding an audience unannounced starts in poor standing. Daimyo total starting Momentum: 3.

**Preparation:** None (the Ronin has nothing to offer in preparation).

**Threshold:** 3 (moderate — permission to operate in the territory is a relatively minor concession, but from a powerful figure to a marginal one).

**The challenge:** The Ronin must overcome 3 starting Momentum before even beginning to gain their own. They choose Scholarly approach — invoking the specific legal precedent that gives investigators rights in murder cases (Knowledge: Law roll). Success on the first roll. They then switch to Cooperative (Socialize) to present their case respectfully.

**Outcome:** The Ronin reaches Threshold 3 narrowly. The Daimyo grants a three-day investigation window with a clan escort (the escort also watches the Ronin). No On gain — Threshold 3 is too modest. But the investigation proceeds.

---

### Scenario D: The Formal Apology

**Setup:** A Bushi (On 12) publicly embarrassed a senior clan member (On 15, Group Rank 4) during a training session — correcting him in front of subordinates. The Bushi must make a formal apology or face ongoing hostility.

**Social Status:** Bushi On 12 (+0) + Group Rank 2 = +2. Senior On 15 (+1) + Group Rank 4 = +5. The senior begins with 1 free Momentum.

**Starting Disposition:** Hostile (the senior was publicly embarrassed). Senior begins with +2 Momentum (Hostile) + 1 (Social Status) = 3 Momentum.

**Threshold:** 5 (significant — a genuine apology that restores the relationship).

**The key mechanic:** The Bushi must not use Adversarial or Authority approaches — these would transform the apology into a confrontation and cost On. Cooperative (Socialize) is the only appropriate approach. The apology itself, if delivered with genuine craft (a Good-quality poem acknowledging the error, prepared beforehand), provides a free Artistic approach for +1 Momentum.

**Outcome:** If the Bushi reaches Threshold 5, the apology is accepted. Both parties gain +1 On (the senior for accepting graciously; the Bushi for apologizing appropriately). The relationship is restored with an obligation bond — the Bushi owes the senior a future service.

If the Bushi fails — reaches 5 Momentum for the senior first — the apology is rejected. The Bushi loses 2 On (the embarrassment stands). A new challenge may follow.

---

### Scenario E: The Police/Yakuza Negotiation

**Setup:** A Doshin captain (On 10, Group Rank 2 in the city government) meets with a Yakuza Oyabun (On 8 criminal On, Group Rank 4 within the organization) to establish the terms of a working arrangement. Both parties prefer this conversation over open conflict.

**Social Status:** The Doshin's formal authority gives them +1 (Authority modifier applies when law enforcement is invoking official power). The Yakuza's Group Rank 4 within their organization is not recognized in formal society — their effective Group Rank for this interaction is 0. Doshin Social Status: +3. Yakuza Social Status: +1.

**Starting Disposition:** Neutral (both parties need something from this; neither is hostile).

**Threshold:** 3 (moderate — a working arrangement, not a formal alliance).

**The dynamic:** The Yakuza uses Streetwise rolls (not on the standard Approach list but approved by GM as a setting-specific Vocational approach) to demonstrate their usefulness as an information source. The Doshin uses Authority sparingly — invoking it too heavily would collapse the negotiation. The Yakuza's Face Edge (criminal On functions normally in underworld contexts) applies: within this negotiation, the Yakuza's On is treated as 14, not 8, because the Doshin knows exactly who they are dealing with.

**Outcome:** The Doshin reaches Threshold 3. A working arrangement is established — the Yakuza provides information on serious crimes; the Doshin looks the other way on gambling and minor vice. No On gain for either party (Threshold 3 is too modest). Both gain a Contact (each other).

## The Gift Economy

Gift-giving in Nippon is a formalized practice with specific rules. An appropriate gift is not a bribe — it is a social statement that acknowledges the relationship, creates obligation, and demonstrates that the giver understands the forms.

### Gift Value and Appropriateness

**Too modest:** A gift below the social station of either giver or recipient is an insult. Giving a Daimyo a bottle of average sake signals either ignorance or contempt.

**Too lavish:** A gift dramatically above the giver's means is embarrassing — it implies the giver cannot manage resources, or worse, that they are attempting to purchase something that should not be purchasable.

**Appropriate:** A gift that reflects careful thought about the recipient's interests, the occasion, and the relationship between the parties. Appropriate gifts need not be expensive — a poem of genuine quality may outvalue a chest of gold in the right context.

### Gift Occasions

| Occasion | Expected Gift | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| First meeting with a superior | Modest, carefully chosen | Establishes respect |
| Seasonal visits (New Year, Obon) | Seasonal goods, local specialties | Universal obligation |
| After receiving a favor | Proportional to the favor | Creates closure |
| Formal petition | Appropriate to the stakes | Covered by Preparation Phase |
| Wedding | Significant | The most important gift occasion |
| Death and funeral | Specific ritual items | Strictly governed by custom |

### The Obligation Bond

When a gift is accepted, an obligation bond is created. The recipient owes the giver a future service of comparable value. This is not legally enforceable — it is socially enforceable. Failing to honor an obligation bond when called upon costs 1d4 On.

Obligation bonds accumulate over a career. A character with many outstanding obligations is considered well-connected; a character with many unpaid debts is considered unreliable.

## Group Rank

Group Rank measures your standing within an organization — clan, temple, Ryu, criminal gang, or city government. It is a number from 0 to 5.

| Group Rank | Status | On Minimum | Resources Available |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | Probationer / Outsider | Any | None — on trial |
| 1 | Member | On 4+ | Basic: lodging, introduction, minor support |
| 2 | Established Member | On 7+ | Moderate: equipment loans, organizational backing |
| 3 | Senior Member | On 10+ | Significant: command of subordinates, formal authority |
| 4 | Leader / Elder | On 13+ | Major: policy influence, substantial resources |
| 5 | Head / Master | On 16+ | Complete: final authority within the organization |

### Advancing Group Rank

Advancement requires:
1. Meeting the On minimum for the target rank
2. Demonstrated deeds appropriate to the target rank (the GM determines when this threshold is met)
3. A successful Tier 3 Social Conflict petition (Scenario A above is the template)

Group Rank advancement cannot be rushed. Between a failed petition and the next attempt, at least one season (three months of game time) must pass.

### Group Rank and Social Rolls

Within an organization, Group Rank differences apply directly to Socialize rolls:

+1 per rank higher than your target (maximum +3)
−1 per rank lower than your target (maximum −3)

This modifier applies in addition to the On modifier.

### Cross-Organizational Status

A character's Group Rank in one organization does not transfer to another. A Yakuza Oyabun (Group Rank 4) has no formal standing in a Samurai clan hierarchy, though their reputation (On) still applies.

Some organizations recognize each other's authority structure. Buddhist temples broadly recognize Shinto shrine ranks and vice versa. Rival Samurai clans do not.

## Social Status and the Buke/Heimin Divide

The most fundamental social division in Nippon is between the Buke (warrior class) and the Heimin (common people). This divide affects every social interaction across the boundary.

**Heimin addressing Buke:** Heimin are expected to use respectful forms of address, defer to Buke judgment, and not initiate social interactions with Buke unless invited. A Heimin who fails to observe these forms loses 1 On. A Buke who is openly disrespected by a Heimin may legally respond with violence, though doing so in front of witnesses costs On if the Heimin's offense was minor.

**Buke addressing Heimin:** A Buke who maintains dignity in interactions with Heimin gains nothing socially — it is expected. A Buke who publicly demeans Heimin gains nothing and may lose On if witnesses judge the behavior excessive. A Buke who treats Heimin with genuine respect is unusual and may gain On if this is part of a consistent reputation.

**Crossing the line:** Social interactions that cross the Buke/Heimin line are always more difficult than interactions within the same class. Apply an additional −1 to Socialize rolls across this divide in formal contexts.

## The Bow

Every significant social interaction in Nippon begins with a bow. The bow is not a mystery — characters raised in Nipponese society know instinctively how they should bow to any given person in any given context. It is not a skill roll. It is not a guess. It is a declaration.

The player states how their character bows. The GM states how the NPC bows. What follows flows from that exchange.

### The Two Components

A bow has two components declared together:

**Level** — who you are treating the other person as:
- **Superior** — I acknowledge you outrank me in this context
- **Equal** — I treat you as my peer
- **Inferior** — I acknowledge you are below me in this context

**Quality** — how you perform the bow:
- **Discourteous** — cold, contemptuous, or challenging
- **Courteous** — the standard, appropriate bow
- **All Courtly Graces** — elaborate, warm, deferential, or honoring

A young Samurai meets another young Samurai of similar standing on the road. The player says: *"I bow equal, courteous."* The GM says: *"He bows equal, courteous."* They pass each other without incident. The same Samurai enters a blacksmith's shop. The player says: *"I bow superior, courteous."* The GM says: *"He bows inferior, courteous."* A normal transaction begins.

### Who Bows First

The inferior person bows first, as a rule. This is itself a social signal — in ambiguous situations, watching who bows first tells you something about how each party perceives the relationship.

### The Correct Level

Characters know how to bow. Nipponese society has clear hierarchies and characters raised within it do not accidentally bow the wrong level. If a player is genuinely uncertain about the appropriate level for a situation, they may ask the GM — "What would be the right bow here?" The GM will tell them, or may say "that would be unusual" if the player's intended bow would read as odd or aggressive in context.

The general principles:

- Buke bow Superior to Heimin; Heimin bow Inferior to Buke. This is the most fundamental social division and is never ambiguous.
- Within the same class, On and Group Rank determine level. Higher Social Status (On modifier + Group Rank modifier) receives the Inferior bow.
- Within the same organization, Group Rank governs regardless of class — a Heimin Yakuza of Group Rank 3 bows Superior to a Buke Yakuza of Group Rank 1 within that organization's context.
- Merchants bow Inferior to Farmers in the formal social hierarchy, but within a shared organization bow by Group Rank.
- Equal On and equal Group Rank means equal bow.

### Bow Quality and Its Consequences

**Courteous** is the default. It carries no special weight in either direction. It is simply correct.

**All Courtly Graces** signals warmth, respect, friendship, honor, supplication, or genuine deference. Done at the right moment it opens doors — the GM may apply a bonus to what follows, shift a starting Disposition toward Friendly, or allow the bow itself to count as preparation in a Tier 3 Social Conflict. Done at the wrong moment — to an enemy, to someone dramatically beneath you in a competitive context, or in a situation where it reads as desperation — the GM may apply a penalty instead. A superior who bows All Courtly Graces to an inferior after a remarkable deed is making a public statement. The GM may award On to the recipient on the spot.

**Discourteous** signals contempt, hostility, disapproval, or challenge. It makes what follows more difficult — the GM will apply penalties to social rolls in the interaction as appropriate. A discourteous bow to a superior is dangerous; a superior may demand an explanation, issue a challenge, or simply have you removed. A discourteous bow from a superior to an inferior is a public humiliation — the GM may impose an On loss on the inferior immediately. Among acknowledged enemies, a discourteous bow carries less weight — contempt is expected and the social sting is diminished.

### The Wrong Level — A Grave Insult

Intentionally bowing the wrong level is far more serious than bowing correctly but discourteously. It is a deliberate challenge to the entire social order.

**Bowing Superior when you should bow Inferior** — claiming status you do not have — is a profound insult to the person you should be acknowledging. It says: I do not recognize your position. The GM should treat this as a serious provocation. Depending on context, it may cause immediate On loss for the person being insulted, trigger a demand for a Formal Duel, or create a lasting social problem that follows the character.

**Bowing Inferior when you should bow Superior** — deferring to someone below you — is rarer but also significant. It may read as deliberate sarcasm, as a calculated power move, or as an attempt to publicly shame the inferior by making their low status theatrically visible. In the right circumstances it can also be a genuine gesture of honor that shocks everyone present.

The insult of a wrong-level bow is difficult to walk back. A discourteous bow can be explained — I was angry, I showed poor judgment. A wrong-level bow is harder to explain without compounding the insult.

### Power and the Insult

Being the insulter is not always the weaker position. A Daimyo who bows Superior discourteous to one of his own retainers in public has issued a statement — and the retainer must decide how to respond. Responding too aggressively to an insult from a superior costs On. Absorbing the insult without response also costs On. The insulter sometimes controls the situation precisely because the insulted has no clean options.

Similarly, a Ronin who bows Equal to a high-ranking Samurai rather than Inferior is making a claim. If the high-ranking Samurai lets it pass without comment, the Ronin has established something. If the Samurai challenges it, a confrontation follows — but the Ronin chose the ground.

The GM should read these situations as social terrain, not as rule violations to be corrected. The rules describe what the bow means. The consequences are always a matter of context, audience, and what the characters do next.

### The GM's Role

The bow system works because the GM plays NPCs consistently. When the players enter the Daimyo's court, every NPC bows in a way that tells the players something — about that NPC's perception of the party, about the social dynamics in the room, about what's about to happen.

**Use bowing to communicate:** An NPC who bows Equal when the players expect Inferior is signaling something. An NPC who bows All Courtly Graces when Courteous would suffice wants something — or is genuinely moved. An NPC who doesn't bow at all has made a statement more powerful than any bow.

**Apply consequences as judgment, not formula.** The bow system produces social consequences — but these are guidelines for GM interpretation, not hard mechanical rules. A discourteous bow in a tense negotiation might cause a penalty to the next Socialize roll, or it might end the negotiation entirely, or it might impress a warlord who respects directness. The bow tells you what the character intended. The GM determines what it meant in context.

### A Note to Players

You are playing characters who grew up in this culture. You know how to bow. When you are uncertain, ask — the GM will tell you what the correct bow is, or warn you that a particular choice would be read as unusual. There are no accidental insults from getting the level wrong. If you bow wrong, you meant to.
