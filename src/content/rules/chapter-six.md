Combat in Savage Bushido uses standard SWADE rules as its foundation. Everything in the Savage Worlds core rules applies — Action Cards, Wild Cards, Bennies, Shaken, Wounds, the works. This chapter covers the additions and modifications specific to Nippon: the weapons and armor of the period, subdual damage, the Formal Duel system, and Mass Battle using the Warfare skill.

When a rule in this chapter conflicts with the SWADE core rules, this chapter takes precedence. When this chapter is silent on a topic, use the SWADE core rules.

---

## The Shape of Violence in Nippon

Violence in Nippon is not random. It is embedded in social structures, legal frameworks, and codes of conduct that determine who may fight whom, with what weapons, and under what circumstances. Understanding this context makes combat more interesting — and more dangerous.

**Legal violence:** A Samurai may legally cut down a commoner who offers grave insult. A Daimyo may order executions. Formal duels between Samurai are legally recognized. These forms of violence carry no legal consequences for the victor, though they may carry social ones.

**Illegal violence:** Attacking a superior, murdering a fellow Samurai without cause, and brigandry are serious crimes. The penalty is typically death, though a sufficiently powerful patron can make legal problems disappear.

**Ritualized violence:** The Formal Duel and seppuku (ritual suicide) are structured forms of violence with specific protocols. Violating those protocols carries social costs that may exceed the physical ones.

## Armor of Nippon

### Armor Encumbrance — Setting Rule

Each die type below the armor's Minimum Strength imposes a −1 penalty to Pace, Agility, and all Agility-linked skill rolls. A character with Strength d6 wearing Heavy Samurai armor (Min Str d10) suffers −2 to Pace, Agility, Fighting, Stealth, Athletics, and all other Agility-based skills.

This penalty applies to the minimum for wearing the armor at all — characters who are too weak simply cannot wear the heavier pieces.

### Social Aspects of Armor

Armor in Nippon is not just protection — it is a statement of identity and status. Wearing armor appropriate to your station is expected. Wearing armor above your station is presumptuous. Wearing no armor at all in a formal military context is either a calculated statement of confidence or an embarrassing oversight.

**Heimin wearing Samurai armor:** Illegal without explicit dispensation. Costs 2 On if witnessed by Buke who care about such things (most do). The armor may be confiscated.

**Samurai wearing no armor in battle:** Not illegal but socially notable. May be read as supreme confidence (adds to reputation) or recklessness (costs On if things go badly).

## Subdual Damage — Setting Rule

Melee attacks may be declared Subdual before the attack roll. Subdual attacks deal Fatigue rather than Wounds.

**Declare before rolling.** You cannot decide to make an attack subdual after seeing the result.

**Melee only.** Ranged attacks cannot be declared Subdual.

**Fatigue results:**
- With a success: 1 Fatigue
- With a raise: 2 Fatigue
- Extras are Incapacitated (unconscious) at 1 Fatigue from subdual
- Wild Cards are Incapacitated at 3 Fatigue from subdual

**Waking up:** An Incapacitated character from subdual damage wakes after 1d6 × 10 minutes, or when a successful Healing roll is made.

**Subdual-default weapons:** Some weapons deal Subdual damage by default — the Jitte, Sai, Tonfa, and Kiseru pipe. These may be used to deal normal damage with a deliberate declaration, but subdual is assumed unless stated otherwise.

**Converting to lethal:** A character who has been Incapacitated by Subdual damage is helpless. A deliberate killing blow against an Incapacitated, helpless character succeeds automatically. Doing so without cause costs 2 On.

## The Formal Duel

The Formal Duel is a structured social and martial institution — a legal method for resolving disputes between Samurai that carries the weight of ceremony and witnesses. Violating the protocols of a Formal Duel is a serious breach of On.

A Formal Duel proceeds in three phases.

### Phase One: The Challenge

One party issues a formal challenge. The challenger announces their lineage (clan and Profession rank), states their grievance, and invites the challenged party to respond.

**Taunt roll:** The challenger makes a Taunt roll. The challenged party may respond with their own Taunt roll (opposed) or accept without contesting.

- **Challenger wins:** +1 to the challenger's first attack in Phase Three, −1 to the challenged party's first attack
- **Challenged wins the opposed roll:** No modifier for either party; the challenger's attempt fell flat
- **Challenged refuses without cause:** Costs 1d4 On. A challenge may be refused with cause — rank disparity, illness, existing obligation — without On loss, but requires a formal statement of reason and typically creates a debt of honor to resolve at a later date

### Phase Two: The Compact

The terms of the duel are agreed before combat begins. Both parties (and their seconds, if present) agree on:

**The stakes:**
- *First blood* — the first Wound ends the duel
- *Submission* — the duel ends when one party yields or is Incapacitated
- *To the death* — the duel ends only in death

**Weapons:** Both parties agree on weapons (typically katana vs. katana for Bushi, but other combinations are possible for Budoka or mixed duels).

**Witnesses:** At least one witness per side is traditional for formal duels. Witnesses confirm the outcome. A duel with no witnesses still happened legally, but may be disputed.

**Violation of terms costs 3 On immediately.** If the duel was to first blood and the winner delivers a killing blow, they lose 3 On regardless of the outcome. If a bystander interferes, they lose 3 On and face a new challenge from the aggrieved party.

### Phase Three: The Combat

Standard SWADE combat applies with the following modifications:

**No Gang Up.** Formal duels are one-on-one. Allies may not assist.

**No Interference.** Any character who interferes in a formal duel loses 3 On immediately and faces a challenge from the aggrieved party.

**Iai-jutsu in sheathed-start duels.** If both parties begin the duel with weapons sheathed (the traditional form), Iai-jutsu practitioners act before all other combatants in Round 1 regardless of Action Card. This is the one situation where Iai-jutsu's timing advantage is decisive.

**Sparing the loser:** When a duel ends by first blood or submission, the winner chooses whether to spare or execute the loser.
- **Spare:** Winner gains +1 On. Loser gains +1 On (for fighting with honor). An obligation bond is created — the loser owes the winner a debt of honor.
- **Execute when terms were first blood or submission:** Winner loses 1 On. The breach of terms is noted by witnesses.

## Seppuku — Ritual Suicide

Seppuku is the ritual suicide of a Samurai — performed to avoid capture and dishonor, to protest an unjust command, or to atone for a serious transgression. It is a significant act with significant social weight.

A character who performs seppuku obviously cannot continue as a player character. This is a narrative endpoint — a choice that the player and GM should approach with appropriate gravity.

**The ritual:** Seppuku requires a Katana or Tanto, a kaishakunin (a second who delivers the beheading stroke to end suffering), and traditionally a death poem composed beforehand.

**Social consequences:** A warrior who chooses seppuku rather than disgrace dies with full On. Their family's standing is maintained. A warrior who is captured and executed (instead of committing seppuku) dies in disgrace. The difference matters to surviving family members.

**GM guidance:** Seppuku should never feel like a mandatory choice. It is an option available to characters who choose it, not a punishment the GM imposes. The existence of this option reinforces the weight of honor in Nippon — the knowledge that death is preferable to dishonor is part of the Buke worldview, not just a rule.

## Mass Battle — The Warfare Skill

When the conflict involves armies rather than individuals, the Mass Battle system from SWADE applies, modified for the Warfare skill.

### Battle Roll

In a Mass Battle, player characters make Warfare rolls rather than generic Battle Rolls. The Warfare skill explicitly represents tactical command capability — the ability to read a battlefield, coordinate forces, and exploit opportunities.

| Warfare Roll | Effect |
|---|---|
| Critical Failure | Lose 1d6+2 Token Points. Disgrace roll: Spirit (TN 4) or lose 1 On |
| Failure | Lose 1d4 Token Points |
| Success | Opposing force loses 1 Token Point |
| Raise | Opposing force loses 1d4 Token Points |
| Two Raises | Opposing force loses 1d6 Token Points. +1 On for valor |

### Champion Challenge

Before the first Battle Roll, either side may issue a Champion Challenge — a call for single combat between named warriors to determine an advantage before the armies clash.

**Accepting:** The named champion fights a Formal Duel (abbreviated to one round of combat if time is short). The winner's side gains +1 to the first Battle Roll (+2 if the victory was decisive — by two or more raises).

**Refusing:** The refusing side's commander loses 1 On. Their side suffers −1 to the first Battle Roll (the army's morale is affected by their champion's reluctance).

### Battlefield Heroics

Player characters may undertake individual missions during a Mass Battle — assassinating an enemy commander, capturing a strategic position, destroying supplies. These are handled as standard adventures running parallel to the battle. Success provides significant Token Point bonuses; failure may contribute to defeat.

## Weapon Legality by Social Class

Nipponese law is explicit about who may carry which weapons. Violations are taken seriously by legal authorities and carry social costs regardless of legal consequence.

| Weapon | Heimin | Ronin | Buke | Notes |

| Tanto | Restricted | Yes | Yes | Heimin may carry for work purposes |
| Wakizashi | No | Yes | Yes | The mark of Samurai status |
| Katana | No | Yes | Yes | The primary mark of Buke identity |
| Nodachi | No | Yes | Yes | Openly carried — unusual even for Buke |
| Yari | Restricted | Yes | Yes | Ashigaru in service may carry |
| Bow | Restricted | Yes | Yes | Hunting bows permitted for Heimin |
| Arquebus | No | Yes | Yes | New technology — laws still forming |

| Farm Tools | Yes | Yes | Yes | Not legally weapons |
| Jitte | Doshin only | No | Doshin | The police truncheon — badge of authority |

**Carrying an illegal weapon:** First offence — weapon confiscated, 1 On loss. Repeat or aggravated — 2 On loss, possible arrest. In hostile or lawless territory these rules become suggestions rather than laws.
