This appendix supplements the Group Rank rules in Chapter Seven with specific modifiers for organizational prestige and cross-organizational interactions. These factors apply when Group Rank interacts with social rolls in contexts where the organization's reputation — not just the character's rank within it — carries weight.

---

## Organizational Prestige

Some organizations carry more social weight than others in specific contexts. A Group Rank 3 member of a major clan and a Group Rank 3 member of a minor provincial clan technically hold the same rank — but the first walks into a room with considerably more authority behind them.

| Organization | Context Where Prestige Applies | Modifier |
|---|---|---|
| Samurai Clan (major, nationally recognized) | Any formal Buke interaction | +1 to Group Rank modifier |
| Samurai Clan (minor, regional only) | Formal Buke interaction outside home region | −1 to Group Rank modifier |
| Imperial Shrine (Ise-level) | Any Shinto formal interaction | +2 to Group Rank modifier |
| Major Buddhist Complex (Nara, Kyoto temples) | Any Buddhist formal interaction | +1 to Group Rank modifier |
| Shogunate (direct service) | Any formal governmental interaction | +2 to Group Rank modifier |
| Yakuza (recognized major organization) | Criminal underworld interactions | +1 to Group Rank modifier |
| Yakuza (minor gang) | Criminal underworld interactions outside home territory | −1 to Group Rank modifier |

These modifiers stack with the standard Group Rank modifier from Chapter Seven. A Group Rank 3 member of a major clan (+3 from rank, +1 from prestige) has an effective Group Rank modifier of +4 in formal Buke contexts — capped at +3 for the standard modifier, but the prestige modifier applies separately.

## Cross-Organizational Interactions

When characters from different organizations interact formally, the following rules apply.

### Recognized Peer Relationships

Some organizations have a baseline of mutual recognition regardless of the individuals involved:

**Buddhist and Shinto clergy:** Treat each other as Group Rank 1 equivalents for hospitality purposes regardless of actual rank. A Buddhist monk of Group Rank 4 visiting a Shinto shrine is received as a guest of some standing, not as a stranger. The reverse applies equally.

**Ryu and Elemental Schools:** A Shugenja of Group Rank 3+ in one School is treated as Group Rank 1 by other Schools for hospitality and access to training facilities — not for technique access, which remains School-specific.

**Rival Samurai Clans (allied):** Clans with formal alliance treat each other's Group Rank normally.

### Hostile or Rival Relationships

**Rival organizations:** Characters from rival clans or competing criminal organizations treat each other's Group Rank as 1 lower for social modifier purposes. This reflects the institutional suspicion and competitive dynamic between rivals.

**Active enemies:** Characters representing organizations currently at war with the target's organization have no Group Rank benefit at all in formal interactions with that organization — their rank means nothing to people who regard their institution as an enemy.

### No Recognition

**Yakuza in formal Buke contexts:** Yakuza Group Rank carries no modifier in formal Samurai clan interactions. A Yakuza Oyabun (Group Rank 5) has no standing in a Daimyo's court — unless their On is high enough and their identity as a criminal is not known.

**Buke in criminal contexts:** Samurai Group Rank similarly carries no modifier in formal Yakuza organizational interactions. A Hatamoto demanding respect from a Yakuza gang in their own territory is asserting legal authority, not organizational standing — use Intimidation or formal authority, not Group Rank.

## Changing Organizations

A character who leaves one organization and joins another carries their On score but not their Group Rank. Earned reputation travels; institutional standing does not.

**Exception — related institutions:** A monk who leaves one Buddhist temple and joins another begins at Group Rank 1 at the new institution but may advance more quickly than a complete newcomer — the GM may reduce the deed requirements for the first advancement by half, reflecting the existing foundation of training and demonstrated character.

**Hostile departure:** A character who leaves an organization under bad terms (expelled, fled under accusation, betrayed their obligations) may find that their former organization actively works against them in their new affiliation. This is a narrative complication, not a mechanical penalty — but it should be real.

---

## Group Rank and On — The Feedback Loop

Group Rank and On reinforce each other in a virtuous (or vicious) cycle. High On opens the door to higher Group Rank. Higher Group Rank provides opportunities for actions that gain On. A character who maintains both climbs steadily; a character who loses either finds both harder to hold.

**The floor:** A character whose On drops below the minimum for their current Group Rank does not automatically lose that rank — but they cannot advance further until On recovers, and superiors may begin assigning them less prestigious duties, which reduces opportunity for On-gaining deeds. The floor enforces itself through narrative pressure before it enforces itself mechanically.

**The ceiling:** Group Rank 5 in any organization represents the pinnacle of that institution. Characters who reach it have achieved something that most people in Nippon never will. The GM should treat Group Rank 5 characters as significant figures in the world — people whose decisions affect the lives of many others, and who carry corresponding obligations.
