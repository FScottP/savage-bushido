Nippon is a land of beauty and blood. Cherry blossoms fall on battlefield corpses. Courtiers compose poems about the transience of moonlight while their lords plot assassination. Peasants farm the same fields their ancestors fought over for generations, paying taxes to whoever's army arrived most recently and praying the next army takes longer to come.

This chapter provides everything a Game Master and players need to understand the world of Savage Bushido: its history, the current crisis that forms the backdrop of any campaign, its geography, daily life, and the practical rules for downtime, advancement, and finding teachers.

---

## A Note on Nippon

Nippon in Savage Bushido is not Japan. The names have been changed — of clans, provinces, and individuals — to create a mythical version of medieval Japanese culture that Game Masters and players can engage with as a fiction. The geography follows the real islands of Japan; the history is inspired by Japanese history but remixed and reordered; the religion draws on Shinto and Buddhist traditions but simplifies and fictionalizes them for playability.

The current conflict driving the campaign is inspired by the English Wars of the Roses — a dynastic struggle between powerful noble families, a weak central authority, ambitious warriors, and the grinding cost of endless war on ordinary people. The specific families and events are original to Savage Bushido, but GMs familiar with the Wars of the Roses will find the broad strokes familiar and useful as a reference.

## The History of Nippon

### The Age of Kami and Kings

When the world was new, the twin gods Izanagi and Izanami stirred the dark waters and raised the islands of Nippon from the deep. Their children became the eight great Kami, and the islands were divided among them to rule. From these divine ancestors came the clans of humanity.

The earliest human history of Nippon is the story of the Wa Kingdom — a tribal confederation that grew into a true state when Grand Admiral Jofuku arrived from Qin with horses, bronze weapons, and ambition. His descendants became the Kings of Wa, and eventually the Emperors of Nippon. From the beginning, the Emperor's power derived from sacred legitimacy — descent from Ohirume, the Sun Goddess — rather than from military force alone.

### The Rise of the Samurai

For centuries, Emperors ruled through the Kuge — the court nobility — who governed the provinces as appointed administrators. Over time, this system hollowed out. Kuge governors preferred the cultural pleasures of the capital to the grinding reality of provincial administration, and increasingly delegated real authority to local warrior families — the Samurai.

These warrior clans grew powerful, then powerful enough to fight each other. Through generations of provincial warfare, most Samurai clans aligned with one of two great lineages descended from Imperial blood: the Genji and the Heike.

The great conflict between these two clans came to a head when an Imperial succession dispute split the court. The Heike won the first war, their leader becoming the most powerful man in Nippon. But his arrogance alienated both the Kuge and the Samurai. When he died, the Genji rose again. The five-year Genji-Heike War ended in the complete destruction of the Heike. The Genji were supreme.

Rather than repeat the Heike's mistake of becoming entangled in the court, Genji Yoritomo had the Emperor appoint him Shogun and established a separate military government at Kamakura. The Shogunate was born.

### The Shogunate — The Age of the Sword

Under the Shogunate, Nippon found a new stability — uneasy, martial, and built on the constant threat of military force, but stable. The Shogun appointed Daimyo as military governors of the provinces. A legal code was established. The Buke were supreme.

Over generations, the Kojitomi clan supplanted the Genji and established their own Shogunal dynasty. They reached their peak under Shogun Monomosa, whose armies won famous victories on the continent. But the Shogunate's strength proved its weakness. A Shogun who is too powerful breeds resentment. A Shogun who is too weak invites ambition.

### The Wars of the Red Samurai

Shogun Monomosa's heir was Tamekura Monokuro — called the Red Samurai for his distinctive crimson-lacquered armor — a brilliant warrior who led Nipponese armies to continental victories. But the Red Samurai died of illness one year before his father.

Monokuro's only son, Tamekura Borukuro, became Shogun at age ten. He was a poor ruler — favoring flatterers, mismanaging the continental campaigns, allowing the Daimyo to run amok. In 1387, a coalition of Daimyo petitioned the Shogun to reform his government. Borukuro refused and ordered them arrested. They defeated the Shogun's forces, took him prisoner, and ruled in his name.

What followed was decades of conflict. The great clans shifted alliances, married strategically, won and lost provinces, and gradually exhausted themselves and the country. The peasantry suffered most. Fields went unfarmed during campaigns. Taxes were extracted by whoever currently held a province. Bandits multiplied.

The current Shogun, Kojitomi Muhide, inherited this chaos at nine months old.

## Nippon Today — The Current Crisis

The campaign of Savage Bushido begins in this moment: a Nippon exhausted by decades of conflict, governed by a weak Shogun dominated by a corrupt Regent, divided between powerful Daimyo pursuing their own interests, and simmering with the anger of veterans who feel cheated and peasants who feel crushed.

### The Power Structure

| Position | Description |
|---|---|
| Emperor (Tenno) | Sacred figurehead, descended from Ohirume. Ceremonial only. |
| Shogun | Military dictator. Currently: Kojitomi Muhide — young, dominated by the Shikken. |
| Shikken (Regent) | Rules in the Shogun's name. Currently: Shibado Kabechika — corrupt, enriching his clan. |
| Kuge (Court Nobles) | Ancient nobility. Culturally dominant, politically marginalized. |
| Daimyo | Military governors of the provinces. Effectively independent warlords. |
| Samurai | Warrior retainers. Serve their clan lord, who serves a Daimyo. |

### Key Figures

**Kojitomi Muhide** — The Shogun, now a young man but still dominated by the Shikken. Married to Lady Jinseong of Kampuchuo as part of a peace treaty many warriors consider humiliating.

**Shibado Kabechika** — The Shikken (Military Regent). Has wielded real power for years. Ended the Kampuchuo war over the objections of the warriors. Has filled lucrative posts with relatives and gutted the standing army. Increasingly corrupt and increasingly hated.

**Kojitomi Yorike** — The Shogun's cousin and a celebrated warrior. Served as General of the Army in Kampuchuo and wanted to continue the war. Was starved of resources by the Shikken. Returned to Nippon angry and ambitious, with hundreds of loyal veterans behind him.

**Ryojii Korisawa** — Daimyo of Mutsu Province. A powerful northern lord who went to war with the neighboring Wada Clan and secured the Shogun's blessing for his campaign. This act deepened the rift between Yorike and the Shikken.

**Wada Kiyoyasu** — Daimyo of the province bordering Mutsu. Under attack from the Ryojii. Wada is Yorike's brother-in-law — which is why the Shogun's blessing of Ryojii's war angered Yorike so profoundly.

### The Fault Lines

**Yorike vs. the Shikken:** Kojitomi Yorike has returned from Kampuchuo with loyal veterans, military prestige, and deep grievances. Whether he seeks to reform the Shogunate, seize the regency himself, or make himself Shogun outright depends on circumstances — and on what player characters do.

**The Ryojii-Wada War:** The conflict between Ryojii Korisawa and Wada Kiyoyasu is a proxy for larger national tensions. The Shogun's blessing of Ryojii represents the Shikken's political favoritism. Wada's connection to Yorike makes this a potential flashpoint for wider war.

**The Disbanded Army:** The Shikken disbanded most of the standing army to save money. Hundreds of trained warriors returned to the provinces with no income and no purpose. Many turned to banditry. Others formed the cores of private armies. Others are available for hire — or for someone to lead.

**The Temples and Shrines:** The great religious institutions have largely avoided taking sides openly, but they have interests. The major Buddhist temple complexes maintain their own armies. Religious legitimacy is a political resource that all factions understand.

**The Heimin Question:** Peasant revolts have become endemic in some provinces. Decades of war, conscription, and extortionate taxation have pushed the farming class toward organized resistance.

## The Land — Geography of Nippon

### Honshu — The Main Island

Honshu is the heart of Nippon — the largest island, where the Emperor's court at Kyoto and the Shogun's seat at Kamakura both stand.

**The North (Mutsu, Dewa, Echigo):** Cold, mountainous, remote. The northern provinces are the least controlled by the Shogunate and the most independently minded. The Ryojii hold Mutsu, the northernmost and largest province.

**The East (Kanto):** The broad plain around Kamakura and Edo. Home of the Shogunate's seat. Heavily militarized.

**The Central Highlands (Kai, Shinano):** Landlocked mountain provinces famous for horses, Yamabushi hermits, and stubbornly independent clans.

**The Capital Region (Yamato, Yamashiro, Omi, Settsu):** The political and cultural heart. Kyoto is here. Omi is the strategic gateway to the capital from the east.

**The West (San'in, San'yo coasts):** The road to Kyushu. Trade routes and sea access make these provinces economically vital.

### Kyushu — The Southern Island

Kyushu has always been the most independent-minded of the main islands and the traditional gateway for contact with the continent. Kyushu clans are proud of their autonomy and suspicious of Kamakura's authority.

### Hokkaido — The Northern Island

Cold, forested, and largely unexplored by Nipponese civilization. Hokkaido is the domain of the Ainu — the Ebisu, as they are called — an indigenous people who speak a different language and follow different customs. Their shamans mix magic and religion around totem animals, especially the bear.

### Shikoku — The Small Island

Small, mountainous, and generally subordinate to the mainland powers. Best known for its great pilgrimage circuit of 88 Buddhist temples.

### Important Cities

**Kyoto:** The Emperor's city — Nippon's cultural, religious, and ceremonial heart. The place whose fate matters most to anyone who cares about legitimacy.

**Kamakura:** The military capital. The Shogunal palace and government administrative offices. Where power is exercised and where ambitious men come to seek it.

**Nara:** The first permanent capital, now primarily important as a religious center. The great Buddhist temple complexes here hold centuries of accumulated wealth and maintain their own armed forces.

**Ise:** The holiest Shinto site in Nippon, dedicated to Ohirume the Sun Goddess. Pilgrims travel from across the islands. Whoever controls access to Ise controls a powerful tool of legitimacy.

**Hakata:** The major port city of Kyushu, Nippon's window onto the continent. Merchants, diplomats, foreign monks, and spies all pass through Hakata.

## Daily Life in Nippon

### The Seasons

**Spring (Haru):** Rice planting. Cherry blossom viewing — a major social occasion at all levels of society. Military campaigns typically resume in spring after the winter pause.

**Summer (Natsu):** The Obon festival — a time when the dead are believed to return and must be welcomed and sent off again. A traditional time for ghost stories and supernatural encounters.

**Autumn (Aki):** Harvest. Moon-viewing ceremonies. The peak military campaign season before winter.

**Winter (Fuyu):** The New Year (Shogatsu) is the most important social occasion of the year — debts are settled, relationships are renewed or ended, and the year's social ledger is balanced.

### Costs and Currency

The standard of living varies dramatically by class. A common retainer Samurai earns approximately 1–3 Ryo per month as stipend.

| Expense | Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Simple meal (rice, pickles, soup) | 1–2 Mon | Common fare for poor Heimin |
| Inn room (common floor) | 5–8 Mon | No privacy |
| Inn room (private, basic) | 2–4 Shu | Decent lodging |
| Inn room (high quality, meals) | 1–3 Ryo | Standard for traveling Samurai |
| Horse fodder and stabling | 5–8 Shu/day | Higher in cities |
| Trained warhorse | 50–150 Ryo | A Bushi's most expensive possession |
| Average Katana | 12 Ryo | Standard retainer issue |
| Physician's visit | 3–10 Shu | Adds +1 to Healing roll |
| Temple purification | 1–5 Ryo | Clears all Pollution |

## Downtime — Between Adventures

Between adventures, characters have time for training, recovery, social activities, and personal projects. The GM establishes how much downtime is available.

| Activity | Time Required | Game Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Study at Ryu or under teacher | 1–4 weeks per Edge | Qualifies to take Bugei or Okuden Edge at next Advance |
| Sunrise Devotion / Shrine Prayer | 30 minutes daily | Full PP recovery for Gakusho |
| Temple or Shrine Purification | 1 full day | Clears all Pollution or Kegare (1–5 Ryo) |
| Craft a weapon or item | 1 day to 1 month | Craft roll determines quality |
| Commission a piece of art | Variable | Three-Layer Art roll |
| Investigate or gather information | 1–3 days | Investigation or Streetwise roll |
| Rest and recover | 1 week | Removes all Fatigue; Wounds heal at standard rate |
| Petition for Group Rank advancement | 1–3 days + prep | Tier 3 Social Conflict (Chapter Seven, Scenario A) |
| Train a skill | 1–4 weeks | May raise a skill one die type with GM approval (costs 5–20 Ryo) |

### Group Rank Advancement — When and How

Group Rank advancement requires both in-game action (demonstrated deeds) and a formal petition. The GM should track whether a character has performed sufficient deeds and signal when the time is right. Between a failed petition and the next attempt, at least one season (three months) must pass.

## Finding Teachers

The Ryu and the wandering master are central to Savage Bushido's advancement system. Okuden cannot be purchased — they require finding the right teacher, earning their acceptance, and committing to the study.

### Methods

**Ask within your organization:** A character with Group Rank 2+ may ask superiors for an introduction to a master. The easiest and most reliable method.

**Reputation and rumor:** Characters with On 13+ may attract teacher interest. Masters seek out students worth teaching.

**Deliberate search (Streetwise or Socialize):** TN 4 for a Tier 1 teacher; TN 6 for a Tier 2 master; TN 8 for an Okuden-capable master. Failure means no contact found in this location.

**Knowledge (Clan Lore/Heraldry):** A character with this Knowledge at d6+ may roll to identify which Ryu or school teaches a specific technique and where they are located.

**Adventure — the wandering master:** Some of the greatest teachers appear as NPCs in the course of play. These teachers require the character to demonstrate worthiness, often through roleplay challenges the GM devises.

### Teacher Costs

| Teacher Tier | Technique | Typical Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Any qualified instructor | Omote Bugei (Tier 1) | 5–15 Ryo per month, or equivalent service |
| Ryu or qualified master | Tier 2 Specialist Edge | 10–25 Ryo per month, or formal pledge of service |
| Ryu headmaster | Zanshin (Seasoned Okuden) | 20–50 Ryo per month, or significant service |
| Ryu headmaster | Other Okuden | 20–40 Ryo per month, or service |
| Named Heroic+ NPC | Shoshin (Heroic Okuden) | Cannot be purchased. Requires demonstrated deeds and a quest. |
| Campaign-level quest | Zanshin Kanzen (Legendary) | Cannot be purchased. A campaign-level story arc. |

### What Teachers Expect

Beyond coin or service, teachers have standards. A master will not teach a student they consider unworthy. Common reasons for refusal:

- The student lacks dedication — distracted by other pursuits or vices
- The student's On is too low — teaching someone of poor reputation reflects on the teacher
- The student has not demonstrated competence in the prerequisite
- The master does not trust the student's intentions

## The Supernatural World in Daily Life

In Nippon, the spirit world is not hidden — it is simply not always visible. Every person in Nippon has had an experience they would attribute to the supernatural: the feeling of being watched in an old forest, the dream that proved prophetic, the prayer answered with uncanny precision.

**Kami are everywhere:** Every natural feature of significance has a Kami. Most Kami are neither benign nor malign — they simply are, with their own natures and preferences. A farmer who thanks the field Kami at harvest and keeps the roadside shrine clean lives in comfortable co-existence with the spirit world.

**Yurei and the unquiet dead:** The ghost story is Nippon's most universal narrative form. Yurei appear whenever and wherever the conditions for their existence persist — unresolved attachment, strong emotion at death, incomplete funeral rites.

**Omens and divination:** No Daimyo makes an important decision without consulting an Onmyoji or Shinto priest for auspicious timing. The GM should occasionally let omens and supernatural signs matter — not as hard prophecy, but as the world's way of communicating with those sensitive enough to listen.

## Adventure Hooks

### Political and Military

- Yorike's agent approaches the party with a secret mission: carry a message to the Wada Daimyo that must not pass through normal channels. If the Shikken learns of it, the war in the north becomes a wider conflict.
- The Shikken's tax collectors have demanded an impossible levy from a small clan. The clan's Samurai have a choice: comply and starve, refuse and be declared rebels, or find a third option.
- A disbanded veteran is terrorizing the roads between two provinces. He is not a simple bandit — he has a grievance. What is it, and does it change what should be done?

### Clan and Social

- A senior retainer of the party's clan is being blackmailed. The evidence may be fabricated. The party must determine the truth before deciding what to do.
- An elderly master swordsman is teaching for the last time, choosing one final student. Several warriors are competing for the privilege. The selection process is not entirely based on skill.

### Supernatural

- A village has been afflicted with unexplained illness and bad harvests since a merchant drove a road through an ancient grove. The Kami of the grove is angry.
- A Yurei has been haunting a castle for three generations. The family has lived with it, mostly. Now it has begun attacking people. Something changed — what?

### Criminal and Underworld

- The Bakuto gang controlling a major city has been fractured by internal conflict. Three lieutenants each claim to be the new Oyabun. The chaos is creating opportunities — and bodies in the canals.
- A Doshin officer asks for the party's help investigating a series of murders in the temple district. The victims are all Shinto priests. The evidence points somewhere complicated.

## A Note to Game Masters

Nippon is a world in motion. The political crisis gives it urgency; the spiritual life gives it texture; the human beings who inhabit it give it warmth. Player characters in Savage Bushido are not observers — they are participants.

The great clans will not sit still while the players are having their adventures. The Shikken will tighten his grip. Yorike will maneuver. The Ryojii-Wada war will escalate or resolve. Run Nippon as a living world that continues whether or not the players are watching, and let their actions actually change it.

The best Savage Bushido campaigns are not about whether a particular clan wins the war. They are about who the player characters become in the process of trying to survive, advance, and occasionally do something honorable in a world where honor is expensive.
